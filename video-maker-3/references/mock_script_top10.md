# Top 10 Candle Making Tricks — narration script (~10 min, ~1,500 words)

Persona: warm, practical candle-maker ("Candice"). One idea per paragraph so the video-maker
can size a B-roll window per sentence/segment. ~150 wpm → ~10 minutes.

---

If your candles keep sinking, tunnelling, or cracking, it is almost never bad luck — it is
one of a handful of small mistakes that every beginner makes. After years of pouring, here
are the ten tricks that fixed every one of those problems for me, in order, from the cheapest
fix to the one that changed everything.

Trick number ten: warm your jars before you pour. Cold glass pulls the heat out of the wax
the instant it touches the sides, and that sudden temperature drop is what leaves you with
those ugly little air pockets and wet spots along the wall. A few minutes with a heat gun, or
even just a warm spot near the stove, and the wax clings to the glass instead of shrinking
away from it. It costs nothing and it is the single most overlooked step in the whole process.

Trick number nine: stop guessing your temperatures and buy a thermometer. Wax has a
personality, and that personality lives in about a fifteen degree window. Pour too hot and
your fragrance burns off and the surface sinks; pour too cold and you get lumps and poor
adhesion. A cheap digital thermometer takes the entire argument off the table. Heat to melt,
let it cool to the pour temperature on your wax's instructions, and suddenly your results stop
being random.

Trick number eight: weigh your fragrance, never measure it by eye. Fragrance load is a
percentage of your wax by weight, not a splash from the bottle. Too little and the candle is
scentless; too much and the oil sweats out and pools on top, which is both a fire risk and a
waste of expensive oil. A small kitchen scale that reads in grams turns your scent throw from
a gamble into a recipe you can repeat every single time.

Trick number seven: add your fragrance at the right temperature, and then stir like you mean
it. Most oils want to be added around one hundred and eighty five degrees, where the wax is
hot enough to actually bind with the fragrance molecules. Pour it in and then stir, slowly and
completely, for a full two minutes. Not a quick swirl — two real minutes. That is what locks
the scent into the wax instead of letting it float on top and flash away.

Trick number six: centre your wick before you pour, and keep it centred while it sets. A wick
that drifts to one side gives you a candle that burns down one wall and drowns itself on the
other. Use a wick sticker at the base, then a wick bar or even a clothes peg across the top to
hold it dead centre. It feels fussy in the moment, but a centred wick is the difference between
an even, clean burn and a lopsided mess.

Trick number five: match the wick to the jar, because the wick is the engine of the whole
candle. Too small and the flame can't melt the full width of the wax, so it tunnels straight
down the middle. Too large and it mushrooms, smokes, and throws soot up the glass. Every wax
and diameter has a recommended wick size — start there, then burn a test candle and adjust.
The wick is not an afterthought; it is the most important decision you will make.

Trick number four: respect the cure time. The day you pour, the candle looks finished, but it
is not. The wax and the fragrance need time to bond and harden together, and for soy that means
one to two full weeks sitting quietly on a shelf. Cure it properly and the cold throw and the
hot throw both come alive. Light it the same night and you will swear the fragrance is weak,
when really it just was not ready yet. Patience is a free upgrade.

Trick number three: fix sinkholes with a heat gun instead of starting over. As a candle cools
it can pull away from the wick and leave a crater right in the centre. Do not panic and do not
re-pour. Run a heat gun gently across the top until the surface just liquefies and re-levels
itself, and the sinkhole disappears like it was never there. One pass, low and slow, and a
ruined-looking candle becomes a flawless one.

Trick number two: always, always burn your candle to a full melt pool the first time. Wax has
a memory. If your first burn only melts a small circle in the middle, the candle remembers that
ring and will tunnel down it forever, wasting half the wax against the walls. The first burn
should go long enough for the melted pool to reach the edge of the glass, all the way across.
Set that memory correctly once, and the candle rewards you for its entire life.

And trick number one, the one that changed everything for me: keep a pour journal. Write down
the wax, the fragrance and its percentage, the pour temperature, the wick, and how the finished
candle actually burned. Because candle making is not magic, it is a recipe, and the only way to
repeat a perfect candle is to know exactly what you did the last time. The makers whose candles
look effortless are not lucky. They simply wrote it down.

So there they are — ten tricks, from warming your jars to keeping a journal. Try even two or
three of these on your next pour and I promise the difference will be obvious. If this helped,
you know what to do, and I will see you at the next pour.
